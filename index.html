A Methodology for Methodologies
Climate-induced Displacement Risk Identification (CiDRI) Framework
An Interconnected Journey: Field Insights → Theoretical Analysis → Practical Tools → Policy Action

Understanding the Interconnected Methodology

This framework demonstrates how each phase organically flows into the next through an iterative, insight-driven process. The table below traces the complete journey where methodological steps generate insights, insights impact the approach, and that impact informs the next phase's methodology. Follow the logical progression to see how 11 key insights across 8 interconnected phases built the comprehensive CiDRI framework.

PHASE 1 Strategic Site Selection & Initial Field Engagement
Methodological Steps
Started with locations experiencing climate stress, deliberately avoiding headline migration areas to prevent bias toward alarmist perspectives
Conducted reconnaissance visits to climate-affected sites to understand ground realities
Studied current broader agreements on displacement, migration and past climate distresses
Engaged with local governments to understand their perspectives, immediate actions and future preventive measures
Key Insights Generated
Insight 1
Governments often have an alarmist perspective when it comes to climate migration. They look at it as a linear process of trigger → loss → migration. Migration storms are seldom the norm, but policies around movement are specifically designed only around migration.
Insight 2
Policies around climate-displacement are generally built for events; hence mobilities are absent from conscious policy making.
Impact on Methodology
These insights revealed the gap between event-based policy frameworks and the continuous nature of climate-induced mobility. This understanding shaped the decision to shift focus from discrete migration events to ongoing mobility states.
↓
Understanding the event-based policy gap led to community engagement to understand mobility as a continuous state of living
PHASE 2 Community-Centered Investigation
Methodological Steps
Engaged directly with communities to understand their coping mechanisms and lived experiences
Identified and documented various types of mobilities emerging from community responses
Classified the types of mobilities observed across South Asia
Segregated and narrowed down study areas based on mobility patterns
Key Insights Generated
Insight 3
Migration is an event, but mobility is a state of living. Mobility could lead to migration or morph into forms like mal adaptation, anticipatory shifting, or relocation and more.
Insight 4
All habitations can be examined for mobility; one should not have pre-defined perceptions on mobility. Any climate stressed settlement can be investigated for mobility patterns and risks at either settlement level or household level.
Insight 5
A single region may be experiencing multiple types of mobilities, even multiple types of climate-mobilities depending upon their current ecosystem.
Insight 6
Immobility could be result of absence of adaptive mobility options. Classic examples are people not directly hit by disasters. In an alarmist and reactive policy scenario, this is worse because policy counts them as 'survivors/non-victims' of a trigger event.
Insight 7
There are significant factors and steps between the decision to migrate and finally migrating. This can include negotiation, partial migration, anxiety.
Insight 8
Current resilience and risk capturing tools are lengthy and difficult to deploy in order to derive field insights on mobility especially at household level.
Impact on Methodology
The recognition that mobility exists as a continuum rather than a binary state fundamentally changed the research approach. Examining only completed migrations would miss the vast majority of at-risk populations. This necessitated developing a framework to capture various states of mobility and immobility.
↓
The mobility continuum discovery prompted examination of existing migration theories to identify gaps between theory and observed ground realities
PHASE 3 Theoretical Analysis & Gap Identification
Methodological Steps
Studied existing theories of migration to understand established frameworks
Analysed gaps between theory and observed ground realities
Identified areas where our research could make unique contributions
Key Insights Generated
Insight 9
Mobility stress (especially forced mobility) can manifest into risks beyond the purview of public policy making, delving into conversations around gender security and mental health.
Insight 10
A house or settlement or even resilience frameworks are not solely enough for capturing mobility trends in a settlement. Policy and governance overlook mobility as there are currently no rapid assessment or evidence capturing frameworks in practice that can predict risks.
Impact on Methodology
Existing theoretical frameworks were insufficient to capture the multi-dimensional risks faced by climate-mobile populations. The need for a tool that could identify risks beyond traditional economic or physical displacement measures became clear. This led to conceptualizing housing ecosystem as a visual entry point for understanding comprehensive community vulnerability.
↓
Recognition of theoretical gaps led to conceptualizing housing ecosystem as the framework foundation for comprehensive risk assessment
PHASE 4 Conceptual Framework Development
Methodological Steps
Developed the theoretical basis for using housing ecosystem as a framework
Conceptualized the relationship between housing, risk identification, and mobility tendencies
Key Insights Generated
Insight 11
Our research focuses on understanding how housing ecosystem can be used as a framework to identify risks to a community from climate change and also its corresponding mobility tendencies. Mobility tendencies shape development. Mobility tendencies are proxies for demands which ultimately shape change.
Impact on Methodology
Housing represents the intersection of environmental exposure, economic capacity, social networks, and adaptive strategies. This conceptual framework provided the foundation for developing the CiDRI matrix as a practical tool that could be applied in the field.
↓
The conceptual framework moved from theory to practical application through tool development and iterative field testing
PHASE 5 Tool Development & Testing
Methodological Steps
Drafted the initial CiDRI (Climate-induced Displacement Risk Identification) matrix
Developed supporting questionnaires for data collection
Conducted rapid testing of the tool in field settings
Analysed which areas the questions were addressing
Identified critical indicators emerging from initial testing
Refined and finalized the tool based on testing results
Validation Insights
Validation Process
The iterative testing process was crucial in transforming the theoretical framework into a practical diagnostic tool. Field testing revealed which indicators were most relevant for identifying both climate risks and mobility patterns.
Impact on Methodology
This process validated the housing-centered approach and refined understanding of how different risk factors interact to influence mobility decisions. The tool was now ready for comprehensive community application to generate real-world data.
↓
The validated tool was applied systematically to capture community voices and develop detailed case studies
PHASE 6 Community Voice Capture & Case Study Development
Methodological Steps
Used the CDRI tool to systematically capture sample community voices
Developed detailed case studies from field data
Generated key insights from community narratives and quantitative data
Data Generated
Rich Qualitative & Quantitative Data
The application of the CiDRI tool in diverse community contexts generated rich qualitative and quantitative data. Community voices provided nuanced understanding of how climate stress, housing conditions, and mobility decisions intersect in daily lived experience.
Impact on Methodology
These insights validated theoretical assumptions while also revealing new dimensions of risk that required further tool refinement. The real-world application identified specific gaps that needed to be addressed in the framework.
↓
Community insights were analyzed to identify tool gaps and accommodate necessary changes for a comprehensive risk assessment framework
PHASE 7 Risk Assessment Framework Improvements (Ongoing)
Methodological Steps
Analysed key insights from community engagement to identify gaps in the tool
Accommodated changes in the CiDRI tool based on field learnings
Created a comprehensive risk assessment framework
Framework Integration
Comprehensive Integration
The iterative refinement process led to a robust risk assessment framework that systematically identifies multiple dimensions of climate-induced displacement risk. Housing vulnerability indicators were integrated with mobility tendency markers, creating a comprehensive diagnostic tool applicable across different climate stress contexts.
Impact on Methodology
The refined framework now provided a robust tool for identifying multi-dimensional risks across different contexts. It was ready to inform practical policy interventions addressing both immediate and long-term challenges.
↓
The comprehensive diagnostic framework connected to actionable policy interventions, completing the journey from field to policy
PHASE 8 Policy Intervention Pathway Development (Ongoing)
Methodological Steps
Analysed community needs identified through the risk assessment framework
Developed intervention pathways based on identified needs and risks
Suggested pilot interventions at selected sites and learn from the pilot results
Created policy recommendations that address mobility as a continuous state rather than discrete events
Policy Translation
From Diagnosis to Action
By understanding the needs revealed through the CDRI matrix, targeted interventions can be proposed that address both immediate climate risks and long-term mobility pressures. This completes the methodological arc from field observation to policy action.
Impact on Methodology
The final phase connects the diagnostic framework to actionable policy interventions. The framework now informs practical policies that address mobility as a continuous state rather than discrete events, fundamentally changing how climate-induced displacement is addressed.
The Complete Interconnected Journey: An Emergent, Insight-Driven Methodology

EMERGENT

Each phase organically built upon learnings from the previous one, creating an emergent methodology that remained responsive to ground realities while developing theoretical and practical contributions.

INSIGHT-DRIVEN

11 key insights progressively shaped the research approach. Field-based observations at each stage led to methodological refinement and new directions, ensuring the framework remained grounded in reality.

GROUNDED

The approach remained responsive to community experience throughout, from initial field engagement through to policy development, ensuring the framework addresses real-world mobility challenges.

The CiDRI matrix emerged as the culmination of iterative learning: from recognizing limitations of event-based policy frameworks → through understanding mobility as a continuum → to identifying housing ecosystem as critical entry point → for comprehensive risk assessment and actionable policy interventions
